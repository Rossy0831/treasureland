Preserving Classic Teochew Puppetry and Opera

Teochew puppetry and opera, once popularly performed during the Hungry Ghost Festival, are gradually being replaced by modern song and dance, as the public's interest in the traditional art form wanes. However, performer Ling Goh remains resolute in her mission to preserve and uphold this fading cultural heritage.


During her childhood, Ling Goh fondly recalls observing her mother getting dressed in magnificent costumes, adorning elaborate headgear, and applying intricate makeup before taking the stage to perform in front of an enthusiastic audience. Behind the scenes, the backstage area became her playground and classroom, where she immersed herself in the art of manipulating iron-rod puppets with skill and delved into understanding the subtle nuances of each character in Teochew opera.


Ling Goh, daughter of Toh Ai Hwa, founder of the Kim Giak Low Choon Puppet Troupe, comes from a lineage of Teochew opera performers spanning back to the 19th century. Teochew puppetry originated as two-dimensional shadow puppets in China, similar to local wayang kulit, using dried leather strips controlled by three long iron rods. During the Southern Song period, the art was brought to Chaoshan by refugees, where it evolved into three-dimensional iron-rod puppets. These Teochew puppets paved the way for the development of Teochew opera. The puppet performers wanted to embody the larger-than-life characters, moving beyond simply manipulating the puppets with their hands.


Ling Goh explains that classic Teochew opera incorporates stories from popular local legends, including romantic tales and ghost stories from the Ming, Tang, and Qing dynasties. The opera is characterized by its grand costumes and elaborate stage make-up, which are essential elements of the performance. The make-up enhances facial expressions and allows the audience to fully immerse themselves in the opera experience. The elaborate headgear and extravagant dresses are designed to create visually captivating scenes and heighten the audience's visual experience.


In Teochew opera, female characters such as the Tragic Woman, Noblewoman, Maidservant, Valkyrie, Old Woman, and Matchmaker can be recognized by the scalloped hairline on their foreheads. On the other hand, male characters consist of the Young Man, Old Man, Fighter, and Comic. The Fool character adds comedic elements to the story, with male Fools distinguished by a distinctive white patch around the eyes and nose, while female Fools use make-up to exaggerate their features for humorous effect.


In recent years, there has been a decline in interest towards Teochew opera, which Ling Goh believes is due to misconceptions that it is solely performed for gods and ghosts. However, she emphasizes that Teochew opera has always been intended for human audiences. She observes that modern entertainment from Western, Korean, and Japanese cultures has captured people's attention, leading them to perceive Teochew opera as outdated (lau beh). Unfortunately, many lack the patience and willingness to learn and appreciate the cultural values embedded within Teochew opera.


Additionally, Ling Goh points out that the practice of temples hiring contemporary singers instead of traditional performers has further contributed to the diminishing interest in Teochew opera. Temples often prioritize attracting larger audiences and increasing devotees, which translates to more financial support for the temple.


Ling Goh continues to perform Teochew puppetry and opera at cultural festivals worldwide. In 2014, she founded the Teochew Puppet & Opera House in Penang, Malaysia, showcasing exhibits of iron-rod puppets, costumes, and props. Visitors can try on opera costumes and learn puppet manipulation. The house also hosts occasional performances, workshops, and talks, preserving the legacy of Ling Goh's family of opera performers across five generations.


Ling Goh expresses her hope that her family's legacy will be carried on by her nephew and niece. Just as she grew up watching her mother perform, the younger generation has grown up observing their grandmother, father, and aunt on stage. The family's tradition has now become their own, with Ling Goh serving as their teacher. She eagerly anticipates their continuation of this cherished family legacy.
