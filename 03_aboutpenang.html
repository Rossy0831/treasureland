About Penang

        Penang is a captivating state in Malaysia known for its rich history, cultural diversity, and natural beauty. Its capital city, George Town, showcases a mix of modern and colonial architecture, with colorful buildings and intricate facades. The city's UNESCO World Heritage Site status highlights its historical and cultural significance. Penang's diverse culinary scene, featuring mouthwatering street food from different cultures, has earned it a reputation as a food paradise. The state's natural landscapes include stunning beaches and the scenic Penang Hill, offering panoramic views of the island. Additionally, Penang serves as an economic hub with a thriving manufacturing industry, attracting multinational companies. Whether exploring historic streets, indulging in delicious cuisine, or enjoying the natural attractions, Penang offers a captivating experience for visitors.

Before 15th Century

          Before Penang obtained its own government, it was part of the state of Kedah, which played a significant role in Malaysian history as an important Indian center of influence and trade. Indian merchants arrived in Peninsular Malaysia before the 1st century, bringing with them herbs, spices, gold, Buddhism, and Hinduism. In the 9th century, Muslim merchants from India and the Middle East arrived, primarily for trade, and played a key role in spreading Islam in the region.


          Chinese merchants discovered Penang in the 15th century, charting the island, which was later named Penang, meaning areca nut in Malaysia's native language. China became an important trading partner through the command of Admiral Cheng Ho, exchanging goods, materials, and techniques with Penang and the rest of the Malaysian peninsula.


          In the 14th century, European merchants, starting with the Portuguese, made their way to Penang, maintaining a monopoly in Southeast Asia by keeping their maps secret. The Dutch arrived in 1595, followed by the English. Francis Light, an Englishman, persuaded the Sultan of Kedah to hand over Penang to the British East India Company. Light founded Georgetown and cleared the dense jungle using a cannon filled with silver coins to motivate laborers. Penang became a prominent trading destination known as the "Pearl of the Orient."

16th Century

          In the 16th century, Penang's history began with the arrival of Portuguese merchants from Goa, India, who were sailing to the Far East in search of spices. They stopped at a small rocky islet on the northern coast of Penang to obtain fresh water from a nearby river. The locals referred to these Portuguese traders as "ferringi." Over time, this rocky islet became known as Batu Ferringhi or Lover's Isle.


          The Portuguese named the main island "Pulo Pinaom," which translates to Betel Nut Island. Later, the name evolved to Penang, meaning "palm tree," possibly due to the abundance of areca nut palm trees in the area.


          During the 16th century, Penang served as a natural harbor for ships from Arabia, China, the Dutch Republic, France, and India. It gained popularity among pirates who targeted vessels passing through the Straits of Malacca. The spice and opium trade between the East and West also flourished during this time.

17th century

          In the 17th century, Penang became a vital natural harbor during the monsoon months for various ships, including those from Arabian, Chinese, Dutch, French, and Indian origins. However, it also attracted the attention of pirates who targeted ships passing through the Straits of Malacca. During this period, both the Dutch and the British sought to dominate the spice trade in the region.


          Captain Francis Light, representing the British, arrived in Penang to establish trade relations. Despite Penang being under the control of the Kingdom of Kedah, the Sultan of Kedah handed over the island to Light in 1771 in exchange for protection against neighboring threats. Light sailed to Junk Ceylon to establish a business, gaining valuable knowledge of local customs and language, which helped him gain the trust of the people.


          In August 1786, Light officially took possession of the island and renamed it Prince of Wales Island. He established a settlement on the northeast peninsula, naming it George Town in honor of the Prince of Wales, who later became King George IV. The population of the previously uninhabited area rapidly grew to 10,000 by the end of the century. Prince of Wales Island flourished as a trading post for various goods, including tea, cloves, pepper, nutmeg, and textiles.


          Meanwhile, Sultan Abdullah of the Sultanate of Kedah, realizing that the British were not providing the protection he sought, gathered an army to retake Penang. However, Kedah was defeated, and Sultan Abdullah entered into a contract with the British, handing over Penang. In return, Light agreed to pay an annual sum of 6,000 Spanish dollars to the Sultan. This arrangement continues today, with the Penang Government paying RM 18,800 to the Sultan of Kedah each year. Following Francis Light's death in 1794, his position was taken over by Martina Rozells and William Light.

18th & 19th Century

          During the 18th century, Penang witnessed the cultivation of various agricultural crops. The fertile land allowed for the cultivation of spices, including pepper, nutmeg, and cloves. These crops contributed to Penang's economic growth and established it as an agricultural center.


          In 1826, Penang, along with Malacca and Singapore, was incorporated into the Straits Settlements, a British administrative unit. This consolidation further strengthened British control over the region and fostered administrative and economic cooperation among the three settlements.


          In the 19th century, Penang experienced significant developments that solidified its position as a British colonial settlement and prosperous trading port. The incorporation into the Straits Settlements in 1826 strengthened British control and fostered administrative cooperation. The influx of Chinese immigrants played a crucial role in Penang's economic growth, while infrastructure development improved connectivity and facilitated trade. The island's economy thrived through trade in commodities such as spices, tea, and tin, and the cultivation of crops like pepper, nutmeg, and cloves. Penang's multicultural society continued to flourish, showcasing the influences of Chinese, Malay, Indian, and European cultures. The establishment of Penang Free School in 1816 contributed to educational advancement.

20th century

          In the 20th century, starting from the year 2000, Penang experienced significant transformations that propelled its growth and development. The state focused on economic diversification, attracting high-tech industries and foreign direct investment. Infrastructure development played a crucial role, with the construction of the Penang Second Bridge and expansions at Penang International Airport enhancing connectivity. Penang's cultural heritage gained global recognition with Georgetown's designation as a UNESCO World Heritage Site, attracting tourists from around the world. The state also saw a booming tourism industry, capitalizing on its natural beauty, culinary delights, and vibrant arts scene.


          Education flourished in Penang, with the establishment of new universities and collaborations with international institutions. The state prioritized sustainable development, investing in green spaces and eco-friendly practices. Penang embraced smart city initiatives, utilizing technology to improve urban services and enhance residents' quality of life.


          Overall, Penang's transformation in the 20th century was characterized by economic diversification, infrastructure development, cultural preservation, and a focus on sustainability and technology. These factors contributed to its reputation as a vibrant economic and cultural hub, positioning Penang as a key player in Malaysia's development and paving the way for continued growth in the future.

Interesting Facts About

Penang

Fun Fact 01

Residents of Penang are know as Penangites.

Fun Fact 02

The name Penang comes from the Malay word Pinang, means the betel nut tree. The original name of Penang was Pulau Ka-satu or “First Island”, but on August 12, 1786 it was it renamed to Prince of Wales Island to commemorate the birthday of the Prince of Wales, who later became George IV.

Fun Fact 03

Penang is known for being the “food paradise” of Malaysia with a wealth of street food or “hawker food” that emphasizes noodles, spices, and fresh seafood. Penang’s cuisine reflects the Chinese, Nyonya, Malay and Indian ethnic mix of Malaysia, as well as influences from Thailand.

Fun Fact 04

Penang is the birthplace of shoe designer Jimmy Choo, the co-founder of Jimmy Choo Ltd that is known for its handmade women’s shoes. Born into a family of shoemakers, Choo’s father taught him the art of shoe-making. Jimmy made his first shoe when he was 11 years old.

Fun Fact 05

The Kek Lok Si-Temple of Supreme Bliss, a sprawling hillside structure, is said to be the largest Buddhist temple in South-East Asia. A funicular train transports you to the summit of the hill featuring a giant statue of Kuan Yin, the Goddess of Mercy.

Fun Fact 06

Penang is the first state in Malaysia to launch the “No Plastic Bag Day” campaign.

Fun Fact 07

Penang is the second smallest state in Malaysia, and is composed of two parts – Penang Island and Seberang Perai on the Malay Peninsula.

Fun Fact 08

Penang was part of the Malay sultanate of Kedah until 1786, when Captain Francis Light built a fort at the site of present-day Georgetown and managed to get the island ceded to the British East India Company. Penang became the first British outpost in Southeast Asia in 1786.

Fun Fact 09

Penang has an equatorial climate with temperatures ranging between 72°F to 86°F and 70-90 percent humidity.
